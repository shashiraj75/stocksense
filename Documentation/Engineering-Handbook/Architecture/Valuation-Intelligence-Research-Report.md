# Valuation Intelligence — Research Report (companion to SSDS-008)

**Purpose of this document:** SSDS-008's Metric Catalogue states *what* each metric is and where its data would come from, and its Methodology Checkpoint compares the *philosophies* against each other. This report states *why* each metric category matters from a financial-research standpoint — the reasoning a future engineer would otherwise have to re-derive. Per this sprint's "evidence over assumptions" rule: every claim is either grounded in standard, widely-taught equity-valuation reasoning or explicitly flagged as this study's own inference, not asserted as settled fact.

---

## 1. Earnings-based multiples (P/E, Forward P/E, PEG, Earnings Yield)

**Why they matter:** P/E is the most widely used valuation shorthand precisely because earnings are the single number most directly tied to what an equity holder actually owns a claim on. Its well-known weakness — that earnings can be temporarily depressed or inflated (a cyclical trough, a one-time gain, an accounting choice) — is exactly why this codebase's existing Business Quality Engine maintains a *separate* Earnings Quality category (Beneish M-Score, Sloan Accruals): Valuation Intelligence is not responsible for judging whether the earnings number itself is trustworthy, only for judging whether the *price paid for it* is reasonable given that it's trustworthy. PEG specifically exists to address P/E's other classic criticism — that a 30x P/E means something very different for a company growing 40%/year than one growing 2%/year — by dividing the multiple by the growth rate. Earnings Yield (1/P/E) is the same information inverted into a percentage, useful specifically because it can be compared directly against a bond yield or risk-free rate, a real and commonly used cross-asset-class comparison technique.

**Confidence implications:** PEG's reliability is only as good as the growth-rate input — this is precisely why SSDS-008's Scope Boundary insists on *reading* Growth Intelligence's own validated growth-rate output rather than re-deriving a second, possibly inconsistent estimate.

## 2. Enterprise-value-based multiples (EV/EBIT, EV/EBITDA, EV/Sales, EV/FCF)

**Why they matter:** P/E compares price to equity earnings, which are affected by how much debt a company carries (more debt means more interest expense, which lowers net income, which raises P/E for an otherwise-identical business). Enterprise Value (market cap plus debt, minus cash) strips capital-structure choices out of the comparison — this is the standard reason EV-based multiples are preferred when comparing companies that finance themselves differently, e.g. a debt-funded utility versus a debt-free software company. EV/Sales is useful specifically for companies without positive earnings yet (early-stage, turnaround) where P/E is undefined. EV/FCF is, by some equity researchers' own stated preference, a more honest multiple than EV/EBITDA specifically because EBITDA's "add back depreciation" treatment can understate real capital intensity — a business that must constantly reinvest in equipment to maintain its revenue isn't actually generating EBITDA-level cash for owners, a distinction EV/FCF captures and EV/EBITDA does not.

**Confidence implications:** This is exactly why SSDS-008 names India's FCF approximation (OCF minus *total* investing cash flow, not isolated CapEx) as a confidence penalty specifically for EV/FCF and Free Cash Flow Yield, not for EV/EBITDA or EV/Sales — the imprecision is localized to the one metric family that actually depends on isolating CapEx.

## 3. Asset-based multiples (Price/Book, Price/Tangible Book, Price/NAV)

**Why they matter, and why they're sector-gated:** for a bank, the balance sheet *is* the business in a meaningful sense — loans and deposits are the actual assets and liabilities generating the bank's economics, so Price/Book is a standard, well-grounded valuation anchor for financials specifically (this is precisely why bank analysts quote Price/Book routinely and software analysts almost never do). For an asset-light business (a software company, a brand-driven consumer company), book value captures almost none of the company's actual economic value — most of that value lives in intangibles (customer relationships, brand, software IP) that accounting conservatism keeps off the balance sheet entirely, or only partially captures via goodwill from past acquisitions. This is the direct, standard justification for SSDS-008's decision to gate Asset-based valuation to the FINANCIAL/REAL_ESTATE sector buckets specifically, rather than applying it as a universal signal the way Earnings-based and EV-based multiples can be.

## 4. Cash-flow-based valuation (Price/Cash Flow, FCF Yield, Owner Earnings, Cash Return)

**Why they matter:** cash flow is harder to manipulate via accounting choices than reported net income (depreciation schedules, revenue-recognition timing, and one-time charges all affect earnings without necessarily affecting cash) — this is the standard reasoning behind preferring a cash-flow-based valuation cross-check alongside an earnings-based one, not instead of it. Owner Earnings is Warren Buffett's own stated refinement specifically meant to separate "cash the business actually generates for an owner to keep" from "cash flow that includes maintenance spending the business has no real choice about" — the maintenance-vs-growth CapEx distinction this concept requires is exactly the data point SSDS-008 confirms isn't cleanly available from either provider, a real, named, universal limitation rather than a market-specific gap.

## 5. Income-based valuation (Dividend Yield, Dividend Sustainability)

**Why they matter:** for income-oriented investors, the yield itself is the primary return driver, and a high yield that can't be sustained (because the payout ratio is unsustainably high relative to free cash flow) is a well-known value trap — a stock that looks cheap on yield alone right before a dividend cut. This is exactly why SSDS-008 treats Dividend Yield and Dividend Sustainability as two separate metrics rather than one: the yield answers "what's the current income return," and sustainability answers "is that return likely to persist," a genuinely different question requiring a different data input (payout ratio, cash generation), not just the yield figure itself.

## 6. Relative and sector-relative valuation

**Why they matter, and why the Methodology Checkpoint rates them highest:** comparing a multiple to *something* (a peer, a sector, the market) is unavoidable in practice — a P/E of 25 means nothing in isolation; it means something specific only relative to what similar businesses trade at. The well-known weakness (an entire sector being collectively overvalued, so every peer comparison still looks "reasonable" within an expensive group) is real but doesn't eliminate the approach's usefulness — it's the reason this study recommends *combining* sector-relative valuation with absolute anchors (historical bands, intrinsic estimates) rather than relying on relative valuation in isolation, exactly the brief's own "do not reduce valuation to simple ratios alone" instruction.

## 7. Absolute/intrinsic valuation (DCF, Graham Formula, Earnings Power Value)

**Why they matter, and why they're explicitly secondary:** these approaches estimate a company's worth from first principles (projected cash flows, a required return) rather than by comparison to anything else — in principle, the most theoretically "correct" approach, since it doesn't depend on the market having priced *anything* correctly as a reference point. In practice, this is exactly why they're also the most criticized in real equity research: a DCF's output is extraordinarily sensitive to the discount rate and terminal growth assumption, both of which are genuinely unobservable and must be assumed — a small, defensible-looking change in either assumption can swing the "fair value" output by a large percentage. This isn't a flaw in this study's own implementation plan; it's a structural, widely-documented property of the methodology itself, which is precisely why SSDS-008's Design Philosophy commits to treating these estimates as a secondary, lower-confidence signal rather than the primary basis for any verdict. The Graham Formula and Earnings Power Value are both, in their own way, attempts to discipline this sensitivity — Graham's formula uses a simple, fixed-form growth adjustment instead of a full multi-year projection; EPV deliberately assumes zero growth specifically to avoid needing a growth assumption at all, trading some realism for a narrower, more defensible assumption set.

## 8. Market-context overlay (interest rates, inflation, growth regime, market cycle)

**Why it matters, and why it's named `[UNAVAILABLE]` rather than approximated:** the same P/E that looks expensive in a high-interest-rate environment can look reasonable in a low-rate one, since equity valuations are conventionally understood to compress when the risk-free alternative (bonds) becomes more attractive, and expand when it doesn't — the entire "equity risk premium" framework in institutional research exists to formalize this relationship. This is genuinely valuable context for a valuation verdict, but this study found no existing provider in this codebase's Data Fabric that supplies the aggregated, market-level historical series (Nifty-level or S&P-500-level valuation history, rate history) this would require — naming it `[UNAVAILABLE]` and recommending a dedicated feasibility spike is the honest, evidence-bounded choice, rather than approximating it with a weaker proxy that could mislead more than it helps, the same standard this engagement applied to Growth Intelligence's own Guidance Consistency gap.
